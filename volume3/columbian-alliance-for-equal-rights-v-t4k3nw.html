TL;DR

In both Fuentes v. Shevin, 407 U.S. 67 (1972), and Calero-Toledo v. Pearson Yacht Leasing Co., 416 U.S. 663 (1974), the Supreme Court emphasized that pre-deprivation process is the constitutional default, and deviations are only allowed in rare, exigent circumstances. Even when exigency justifies skipping pre-deprivation procedures, the state must still offer prompt and meaningful post-deprivation review. Here, the Government offers neither: no hearing before the destruction of property and no mechanism after.

Even in Fuentes, a judge reviewed the request before property was seized. And even if some narrow exigency exception were available, a blanket gang designation followed by officers actively hunting down specific individuals for demolition of their belongings is hardly the kind of fast-moving, unforeseen emergency that justifies suspending constitutional safeguards.
