Deverus 2024-12-09 09:17 p.m.
Prosecutorial Misconduct covers a wide swath of topics, and is well addressed by a variety of caselaw. Brady addresses the failure to disclose exculpatory evidence, Napue addresses use of false testimony to secure conviction, Berger addresses trial misconduct, and the list goes on. Not among the various kinds of prosecutorial misconduct is the amendment of an indictment or information in alignment with the procedural norms of the jurisdictional court. The claim and motion for the dismissal of this case with prejudice for the claim of prosecutorial misconduct due to the presentation of a superseding indictment is, put lightly, a far-fetched abstraction of the facts coupled with speculation and absolutely no founding in any legal precedence whatsoever.

To cement this as fact, we need look no further than Ex Parte Bain, 121 U.S. 1, 7 S. Ct. 781 (1887), one of the very first cases where the issue of superseding indictments was presented, the Supreme Court held that "when this indictment is filed with the court no change can be made in the body of the instrument by order of the court, or by the prosecuting attorney, without a re-submission of the case to the grand jury." In layman's terms, when a substantive change to an indictment or information is made, it must return to a grand jury if the case is to move forward. While the case in Bain's case was ultimately dismissed, it clearly outlines that a superseding indictment of substantive change need be returned to a grand jury- otherwise the case cannot continue.

For better or worse, the State of Mayflower does not utilize grand juries- for cases of a felony or misdemeanor nature. To that extent, we utilize probable cause hearings, found in misdemeanor cases in the real world, to move the wheels of justice along and keep the ball rolling. To meet the standard of certifying the superseding indictment, this court would need return such an indictment back to its probable cause hearing stage- a procedure which was already underway. In this respect, the Defendant's 5th Amendment right is being upheld.

Furthermore, the Defendant's 6th Amendment right to a fair trial is also being upheld. United States v. Rojas-Contreras, 474 U.S. 231, 106 S. Ct. 555 (1985), while addressing a superseding indictment of a far less substantive change, notes that “when an indictment is dismissed on motion of the defendant, and the defendant is thereafter reindicted, both the 30-day and 70-day periods run anew.” In other words, the pre-trial period, during which the defense prepares their case, restarts.

Not only has the original indictment not been dismissed, but these proceedings haven't even made it to the pre-trial period- the defendant himself has not even been issued summons. To assert that a superseding indictment, which has followed every norm and procedure of this court with due respect to the defendant's rights, prejudices and misleads the defendant prior to the defendant being so much as summoned or arraigned, similar to the defendant's motion to dismiss, is a far-fetched abstraction of the facts with no legal precedence.
