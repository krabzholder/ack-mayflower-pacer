walter! 2024-12-27 12:02 p.m.
Thank you, your honor. The State would like to note that the vacatur of arrests, while being by motion, is technically a ministerial act. The relevant code is, as you identified, 3 M.S.C. 1 § 8221, which reads that:

"[t]he Attorney General may through motion to vacate an arrest or other form of detention on the basis that there exists insufficient probable cause for such arrest or attention. . ."

Which is what has happened here, as represented by @Fish. The section furthermore goes on to state:

". . .[n]o court shall review the contents of the vacature order nor deny or any reason except insofar as to determine if the motion is in proper form and certified by the Attorney General."

Which makes this act ministerial.

Fundamentally, there is no authority for the court to exercise discretion here unless it is with respect to the form or certification of the motion (which is not in dispute within this matter). The court, respectfully, cannot reserve its judgement on this matter because there is no discretion to be exercised here. The State asks that a ruling therefore be entered upon the motion before the court, which relates to the petition on this matter, without further delay.

walter! 2024-12-27 03:33 p.m.
The court has no authority to toll a ruling on the motion, and even the provisions of law which govern habeas proceedings so dictate. It is clear that "The State may refuse to defend the respondent in a matter of a Habeas Corpus petition, if the State issues an order vacating the arrest which was the basis of the petition filed." 5 M.S.C. 1 § 1403.1(d). That order was issued, pursuant to the Attorney General's authority in 3 M.S.C. 1 § 8221, as noted before.

The question at hand in this matter is one of habeas corpus. It is clearly cut law that when a matter of habeas corpus is not defended, that the relief be granted without further question. That is the nature of judgements in default, your honor—habeas corpus as a non-criminal matter is bound by these general principles of civil law.

There is no question as to separation of powers here because the legislature, which controls both this court's power and my department's power, has outlined explicitly where these powers are arranged in relation to one another. It is the right of the State to refuse to defend a matter, just as any person may refuse to defend a matter filed against them, only that the legislature has mandated an extra step for the State, on refusal. We have taken that step. It is now squarely in the your honor's court (pun intentend) to promptly dispose of this simple matter of business before the court pursuant to your third canon.

There is no discretion to be exercised and the court must vacate the arrest.
