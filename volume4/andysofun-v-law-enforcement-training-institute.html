Toby Ellis, Esq. 2025-08-16 09:17 p.m.
Ok. Id like for the Court to reconsider the issue of sovereign immunity if you will. Sovereign immunity is the default rule in Mayflower law. 5 M.S.C. 2 1401 says the State, its agencies, and employees dont waive immunity unless a statute says so explicitly. And 5 M.S.C. 1 3202.1 backs that up, saying the government is immune from all claims except where liability is explicitly created.

The statute the Plaintiff relies on, 3102.3, says any individual who fails to perform a contractual obligation shall be liable for breach of contract and shall be liable for punitive damages up to $150,000. Thats the problem it talks about individuals, not the State. Under 1401 and 3202.1, waiver has to be express, and general wording isnt enough. Since 3102.3 doesnt mention the State at all, it cant be read as a waiver.

On top of that, if you read 3102.3 as applying to the State, it collides with 3202.2 and 32033205, which say the State can only ever be liable for actual economic loss and never punitive damages. But 3102.3 allows punitive damages up to $150,000. That flat-out contradicts the immunity provisions, which is why 3102.3 cant have been meant to apply to the State.
