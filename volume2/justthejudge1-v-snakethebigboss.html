A question of law is whether there was a sign and what it said. Whether the provided notice was "appropriate" or otherwise legally sufficient is for this Court to decide.

Undisputed material facts is not disputed.

Plaintiff holds that the sign's stipulation that one is "near the military exclusion zone" and not "in it" is somehow an indication that the MEZ does not start behind the sign. This seems frivolous. First. Of what good is a sign that lets you know you're in a restricted area once you had already trespassed, committed a crime? It goes against common sense. If the sign does not indicate the boundary of the MEZ, where is it actually? If this Court were to hold that the fence is the boundary, then it rules one of the statutory provisions redundant, something that it is not permitted to do.

The argument that the "Federal Property" sign is where the MEZ starts is unwarranted and unsupported by any provisions.
