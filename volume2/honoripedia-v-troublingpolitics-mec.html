MINUTE ORDER ON PROPOSED INTERVENOR BLCVLCI'S MOTION TO INTERVENE

UPON CONSIDERATION of Proposed Intervenor BlcVlCI's Motion to Intervene, for the reasons that follow, IT IS ORDERED that the Motion be, and it hereby is, DENIED IN FULL.

BlcVlCI asserts a right to intervene in this matter or, in the alternative, argues that the Court should allow them to permissively intervene. Mayfl. R. Civ. P. 19 allows a party "claiming an interest in pending litigation ... to assert a right by intervention," but is, regrettably, sparse on details. The Court thus finds Fed. R. Civ. P. 24(a) to be instructive. Under Rule 24(a), intervention is of right if: "(1) the motion is timely; (2) the putative intervenor asserts an interest related to the property or transaction that forms the basis of the controversy in the action into which he seeks to intervene; (3) the disposition of the action may impair or impede his ability to protect that interest; and (4) it is not adequately represented by the existing parties." Chenevert v. Travelers Indem. Co., 746 F.3d 581, 585 (5th Cir. 2014) (citing Effjohn Int'l Cruise Holdings, Inc. v. A & L Sales, Inc., 346 F.3d 552, 560 (5th Cir. 2003)); accord Cabazon Band of Mission Indians v. Wilson, 124 F.3d 1050, 1061 (9th Cir. 1997), cert. denied, 524 U.S. 926 (1998).

The Court concludes that Proposed Intervenor BlcVlCI's Motion to Intervene fails twofold. The Motion is untimely and, in any case, does not properly explain or show how Proposed Intervenor's interests are not adequately represented by the existing parties. It also advances no (meaningful) new arguments; the distinction between "filed" and "certified," within the meaning of Mayfl. Const. art. XI, 3, cl. 3, is a red-herring. Similarly, as Proposed Intervenor seeks to raise new claims or issues against the existing parties, permissive intervention is not proper. See Arizona v. California, 460 U.S. 605, 614 (1983).
